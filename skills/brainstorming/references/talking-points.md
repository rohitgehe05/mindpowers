# Talking Points Template

For verbal delivery prep: OKR defence sessions, townhall Q&A, board questions, podium moments, panel appearances. Different from a decision-doc or briefing-doc because the output is spoken, not read.

The spec optimises for memorability under pressure.

## Sections

| Section | What goes here |
|---|---|
| Setting | Where will this be delivered? Audience size, format, who's in the room, time available. |
| Core message | One sentence you want every person in the room to remember. |
| Predicted questions | 5-10 questions you expect, ranked by likelihood and difficulty. |
| Pithy responses | One line per predicted question. The thing you'd actually want to say, not the official statement. |
| Bridging lines | Phrases to redirect from a difficult question back to your core message. |
| Avoid saying | Words, phrases, or topics that will get misquoted, taken out of context, or escalate. |
| Rehearsal notes | Things to physically practise before delivery. Pauses, tone shifts, eye contact, where to slow down. |

## Elicitation prompts

When self-shaping, one at a time:

1. What's the setting? (Who, what, where, time available.)
2. What's the one sentence you want everyone in the room to remember?
3. What 5-10 questions do you expect, ranked by likelihood and difficulty?
4. What's the toughest question, and what's your honest one-line answer?
5. What words or phrases must you avoid? (For risk reasons or for clarity.)

When a template match is suspected (user said "talking points", "what should I say", "OKR defence prep", "Q&A prep"), batched:

> "Quick frame: what's the setting, what's the one sentence you want everyone to remember, and what are the 5 toughest questions you're expecting?"

## Standards baked in

- **One core message.** Not three. Audiences remember one thing.
- **Pithy beats official.** Write the line you'd actually want to say, then sand off the parts that would get you in trouble.
- **Rank questions by likelihood and difficulty.** Prepare for the hardest, expect the most likely.
- **Bridging lines explicit.** Pre-rehearsed bridges are how you stay on message under pressure.
- **Avoid-saying list.** Naming the trap reduces the chance of falling in.

## Anti-patterns

- Multiple core messages that compete for attention
- Drafting "official statement" answers that no one would actually say out loud
- Skipping rehearsal notes because they feel beneath the doc (they're often the most useful part)
- Preparing only for friendly questions
- Treating this as a written deliverable instead of verbal prep
