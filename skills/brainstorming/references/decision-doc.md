# Decision Document Template

For strategic arguments where you're recommending a position. OKR defence, build-vs-buy, prioritisation memos, organisational changes, vendor selection.

## Sections

| Section | What goes here |
|---|---|
| Decision required | One sentence. What is the decision being requested, who from, by when? |
| Recommendation | The recommended option, stated up front. One sentence. |
| Context | 2-4 bullets. Background a reader needs to evaluate the recommendation. Resist over-explaining. |
| Options considered | 2-3 options, each with a one-line description plus key trade-offs. |
| Reasoning | 2-4 bullets. Why this option beats the others. Argue from criteria, not from preference. |
| Risks and mitigations | 1-3 bullets. The strongest counter-arguments, and how you'd address them. |
| Asks | What you need from the decision-maker beyond approval. |

## Elicitation prompts

When self-shaping (open-ended task), one at a time:

1. What's the decision being requested? One sentence. Who's the decision-maker?
2. What's the minimum context the decision-maker needs?
3. What 2-3 options should we evaluate? (Or: what's the obvious option you're recommending against?)
4. What criteria should this decision be judged on?
5. What's the strongest counter-argument to your recommendation?

When a template match is suspected (e.g., user said "I need to make a decision on X"), batched:

> "Quick check: who's the decision-maker, what are the 2-3 options on the table, what's the deadline, and what's your current lean?"

## Standards baked in

- **Recommendation up front.** Not at the end. Decision-makers want the answer, then the reasoning.
- **Argue from criteria, not preference.** Recommendations rooted in stated criteria are more defensible.
- **Engage the strongest counter-argument.** Do not strawman the alternatives.
- **One sentence on the decision.** If you can't state the decision in one sentence, it isn't crisp enough.
- **Risks named, not hidden.** Every recommendation has risks. Surfacing them is a strength, not a weakness.
- **Source attribution where applicable.** If the reasoning draws on established frameworks, mental models, or prior decisions in the org, cite. Adds defensibility.

## Anti-patterns

- Burying the recommendation under the reasoning
- Listing 5+ options when 2-3 cover the real space
- Strawmanning alternatives to make the recommendation look obvious
- Pretending there's no risk
- Treating the decision doc as a status update (it's an ask, not a report)
